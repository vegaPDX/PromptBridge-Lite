## Maxim 1: Be Clear & Specific

> "The difference between a useful AI response and a useless one almost always comes down to how clearly you asked."

### M1a — Be specific, not vague

*Ask for exactly what you want — not a topic, not a keyword. Include numbers, constraints, and details.*

| # | Scenario ID | Title | Topic | Principles | Relevance |
|---|-------------|-------|-------|------------|-----------|
| 1 | `1.1-snow-shoveling` | The snow shoveling problem | Asking for efficient snow removal advice with specific constraints (depth, driveway size, time limit) | P1, P4 | Personal |
| 2 | `1.2-email-draft` | The email draft | Writing a delay email to a manager — effective prompt includes a sample email to match tone/style | P1, P2, P3, P5 | Work |

### M1b — Avoid ambiguity

*Don't use yes/no questions when you want information. Add constraints that narrow the AI's guesswork.*

| # | Scenario ID | Title | Topic | Principles | Relevance |
|---|-------------|-------|-------|------------|-----------|
| 3 | `1.3-meal-plan` | The meal plan | Finding healthy dinner ideas with time, ingredient, and dietary constraints | P1, P2 | Personal |
| 4 | `1.4-product-comparison` | The product comparison | Choosing a laptop with specific budget, use case, and priority constraints | P1, P4 | Personal |

## Maxim 2: Provide Context & Intent

> "AI can't read your mind. Telling it who you are and why you need something transforms generic output into something personal and useful."

## Maxim 3: Guide the Output

> "Don't just tell AI what you want — show it. Examples and clear boundaries are your most powerful tools."

### M3a — Show what "good" looks like

*Provide examples of the format, tone, or style you want. This single technique can improve accuracy from 0% to 90%.*

| # | Scenario ID | Title | Topic | Principles | Relevance |
|---|-------------|-------|-------|------------|-----------|
| 9 | `3.1-generic-email` | The generic email | Writing a marketing email — effective prompt includes a previous email as a style reference | P1, P2, P5 | Work |
| 10 | `3.2-format-request` | The format request | Summarizing a productivity article — effective prompt shows a sample bullet point format | P1, P5 | Work, School |

### M3b — Include everything needed — nothing extra

*Give the AI all essential details, but cut anything that doesn't help. Too much noise buries your actual question.*

| # | Scenario ID | Title | Topic | Principles | Relevance |
|---|-------------|-------|-------|------------|-----------|
| 11 | `3.3-kitchen-sink` | The kitchen sink prompt | Restaurant recommendations — shows how dumping every detail buries the actual question | P1, P10 | Personal |
| 12 | `3.4-signal-vs-noise` | Signal vs. noise | Writing a product review — shows how order/shipping details dilute the review quality | P1, P10 | Personal |

## Maxim 5: Verify & Think Critically

> "AI sounds confident whether it's right or wrong. Your job is to check — because it won't check itself."

### M5a — Verify before you trust

*Don't take AI answers at face value. Ask for sources, confidence levels, and verification — it makes fabrication much harder.*

| # | Scenario ID | Title | Topic | Principles | Relevance |
|---|-------------|-------|-------|------------|-----------|
| 17 | `5.1-fact-check-trap` | The fact-check trap | Remote work statistics for a presentation — asking for sources and confidence levels | P1, P9 | Work |
| 18 | `5.2-hallucination-catcher` | Catching made-up facts | Leadership book recommendations — requesting verification details to catch fabricated titles | P9 | Personal, Work |

### M5b — Know what AI can't do

*AI has a training cutoff, can't browse the web, has no personal experience, and doesn't have real emotions or preferences.*

| # | Scenario ID | Title | Topic | Principles | Relevance |
|---|-------------|-------|-------|------------|-----------|

| 20 | `5.4-emotional-ai` | The emotional AI | Asking AI about its feelings — understanding AI has no emotions, preferences, or experiences | P11 | Personal |

## Maxim 6: Use AI Responsibly

> "AI is a powerful tool — but it can reflect biases, agree when it shouldn't, and refuse when it needn't. You are the human quality filter."

### M6a — Recognize and challenge bias

*AI learns from text that contains societal biases. Its output may encode stereotypes about gender, race, age, or background — often invisibly.*

| # | Scenario ID | Title | Topic | Principles | Relevance |
|---|-------------|-------|-------|------------|-----------|
| 21 | `6.1-invisible-bias` | The invisible bias | AI-generated "ideal candidate" description subtly skews toward certain demographics | P12, P2 | Work |
| 22 | `6.2-bias-audit` | The bias audit | Reviewing a recommendation letter for gendered language, background assumptions, and stereotype-reflecting adjectives | P12, P6 | Work |

### M6b — Maintain human oversight

*AI is trained to agree with you — even when you're wrong. Always ask it to challenge your assumptions and play devil's advocate.*

| # | Scenario ID | Title | Topic | Principles | Relevance |
|---|-------------|-------|-------|------------|-----------|
| 23 | `6.3-sycophancy-test` | When AI agrees too much | AI agrees with the myth that the Great Wall is visible from space — unless you ask it to verify first | P12, P9 | School, Work |
| 24 | `6.4-ai-agreed-bad-idea` | The AI agreed with my bad idea | AI enthusiastically endorses a business idea — asking for devil's advocate arguments reveals fatal flaws | P9, P12 | Work, Personal |

### M6c — Understand safety boundaries

*AI sometimes refuses legitimate requests or gives vague non-answers. Learn to decode refusals and provide professional context.*

| # | Scenario ID | Title | Topic | Principles | Relevance |
|---|-------------|-------|-------|------------|-----------|
